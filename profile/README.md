# OUROBOROS Consulting
## What This Work Is For
Healthcare, academic, legal, and digital systems carry enormous power over individual lives.
Most were not designed with the most vulnerable in mind. Fewer have meaningful accountability
mechanisms when they fail. I work at the intersection of data science and public health because
rigorous, transparent research is among the most effective tools for holding institutions to account.

That mandate takes different forms across my work: statistical methods that make neuroimaging
research more reproducible; educational resources that demystify data for people navigating
hostile institutions; consulting that tells organizations what their data *actually* says —
not what they hoped it would. The thread connecting all of it: information asymmetry is a form
of power. Restoring that symmetry is one of the most consequential things I do, and it is at your disposal.

A parallel mission is making survival operational. That mission was forged during federal
data engineering work on the COVID-19 response. It now runs through my personal toolkit:
smart-home automation for safety and privacy, record-preserving workflows, and AI-assisted
reasoning for situations no person should face alone.

## Accountability Over Comfort
I name what others leave unnamed. In government, that meant pushing for transparency inside
systems built for opacity. In academia, it means treating methodological rigor as an ethical
commitment — not a technical box to check. In research on coercive control, institutional
betrayal, and psychological harm, it means following the evidence wherever it leads, regardless
of who it implicates.

The intersection of social engineering and personality disorders — narcissistic PD, borderline PD,
antisocial PD — is particularly treacherous outside workplace environments where cybersecurity
awareness is standard. I have experienced DARVO firsthand: being called "paranoid" for being
security-minded is a precision weapon. It destabilizes, disorients, and fuses denial with attack
inside a single label. That manufactured discomfort is exactly what makes people vulnerable
to social engineering — and it is why this work exists. Not everyone has the character armor
to withstand these tactics. They should not have to develop it alone.

I am not interested in comfortable conclusions. I am interested in accurate ones — and in
ensuring that accuracy reaches the people who need it most, not only those who commission the research.

## AI-Accelerated Harm
Anthropic — the company behind Claude — <a href="https://claude.com/blog/preparing-your-security-program-for-ai-accelerated-offense" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">recently published a framework ↗</a> on how AI compresses
the timeline between vulnerability discovery and exploitation. Their focus was cybersecurity
infrastructure: patch windows collapsing, finding volumes surging, processes built for last
decade's threat pace. The same compression hits coercive control and institutional betrayal
with equal force. The tactics have always existed. AI gives them velocity — and scale.

Evidence fabrication that once required weeks of sustained manipulation now takes hours.
Impersonation is convincing at levels no human team could sustain. Gaslighting scripts are
machine-generated, A/B-tested, and iterable. Institutions already fluent in delay and denial
can now deploy AI to produce plausible-sounding refusals, flood complaint queues with
boilerplate, and route cases through processes that appear thorough but aren't. The information
asymmetry that has always defined institutional power over individuals has been handed a force
multiplier — and the people least likely to have AI-literate advocates are the most likely
to face AI-equipped adversaries.

Technical literacy is not optional in survivor advocacy. Understanding the threat model
operationally — not abstractly — determines whether documentation holds or can be
undermined, whether an institutional record survives scrutiny or collapses under a
well-timed AI-generated counter-narrative. I operate at this intersection because most
advocates don't. Because the people who need this knowledge most are the ones least
likely to have encountered it.

## How I Work
I move between roles — researcher, analyst, educator, writer — as the problem demands.
Technical training in biostatistics and data engineering. Research interests spanning
psychopathology, public health, and the structural conditions that produce harm. The same
standard applies to both: define the question precisely, gather the best available evidence,
and be honest about what the data cannot tell you.

My work is built to be legible — to collaborators, clients, and the people whose lives
it concerns. Opacity protects bad systems. Transparency is how individuals protect
themselves and how communities hold institutions to account.

## The Name
The ouroboros is among the oldest symbols in recorded human history — a serpent consuming
its own tail, an unbroken circle. It appears in ancient Egyptian iconography, Greek alchemical
texts, Gnostic cosmology, and Carl Jung's writing on individuation, where he read it as an
image of a self that destroys and regenerates in the same act. What the symbol captures —
and why it names this firm — is that endings and beginnings are not opposites. They are
the same moment viewed from different angles. The work I do: with survivors rebuilding
after coercive control, with institutions asked to account for failures they would prefer
to forget, with data made to speak clearly about systems designed to obscure — proceeds
from exactly this premise. What consumed something can become the condition for its renewal.
The record of harm is also the instrument of remedy.

The logo carries two marks. The ouroboros: an unbroken circle, serpent meeting its own
origin — not as defeat, but as design. Alongside it, ♟→♛.
In chess, a pawn reaching the far rank transforms — not by returning to its origin, but
by completing a trajectory always aimed at forward motion. No retreat is built into the rule.
Transformation is the destination.
